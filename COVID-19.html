Dynamics of SARS-CoV-2 and host immune response

SARS-CoV-2 infection has sickened more than 400 million people worldwide, and killed almost 6 million. For the sake of clinical treatments and global health, it is important to determine how immune response affects the infection severity of SARS-CoV-2 and the different roles of innate, adaptive immunity and immune memory in it. The viral infection process of SARS-CoV-2 shares similar time scale as the ramping of immune response, therefore calls for detailed modeling of the immune system dynamics to readily tackle this urgent medical issue. We used 32-variable ordinary differential equations depicting the dynamics of viral load, immune cell density in local infection area, cytokine and antibody level, to understand the dynamic interplay between immune response and viral infection.

Analytical derivation based on reproduction number Rt reveals the immune system fights against the viral infection non-additively. Instead, the effective strength of immune response is the mathematical product of cellular and humoral immune response, in other words, product of effector T cell density and neutralizing antibody level. Numerical simulation and our analysis of clinical data confirmed faster and stronger immune response correlates with less severe symptoms.

In terms of immune protection by vaccination, the same rule holds. Individual's protection is determined by vaccine-elicited T cell response and antibody response, which provides us a metric to predict vaccine's protection efficacies based on their phase 2 clinical trials of immunogenecity data. Altogether, our quantitative insights reveal the general principle between immune response strength and viral infection severity that could guide the formulation of targeted therapy, and could guide the rational design of next-generation vaccines.
